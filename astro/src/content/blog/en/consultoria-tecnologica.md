Most companies make important technology decisions with little information: which management platform to implement, whether it makes sense to develop something custom or use an existing tool, how to migrate systems without disrupting operations.

Those decisions have consequences that last for years. And making them badly costs much more than making them well would have.

## What technology consulting should do

Useful consulting does not start by recommending tools. It starts by understanding the business: how it works today, what problems it has, what it wants to achieve and in what timeframe.

With that foundation, the work is to translate business objectives into technically sound decisions. What makes sense to build, what makes sense to buy, what order of priority has the most impact.

The deliverable is not a long report. It is a clear roadmap, with evaluated options and a well-founded recommendation.

## When it has the most value

The moment when consulting adds the most value is before making a major decision: before contracting an ERP, before starting the development of a platform, before a significant investment in infrastructure or tools.

It also makes sense when something is not working and it is not clear why: a system that creates more problems than it solves, an integration that always causes issues, a technical team that is not making progress.

## The difference between consulting and an interested vendor

A consultant with no interest in selling you anything specific can give you a more honest diagnosis than a vendor who wants you to buy their solution. That does not mean vendors are dishonest, but incentives do influence recommendations.

When consulting and execution are done by the same team, the key is that the diagnosis is done before committing to anything, and that the evaluated options include alternatives that do not involve hiring the same team.
