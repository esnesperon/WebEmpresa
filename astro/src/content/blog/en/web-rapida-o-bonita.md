When a company comes to us with a slow website, there is almost always the same story behind it: the site was built years ago by someone using a content management platform loaded with plugins, and over time it got slower as more things were added.

The problem is not the design. The problem is the architecture.

## Why websites become slow

Most current corporate websites load dozens of resources the visitor never sees or uses: third-party plugins, entire libraries for minimal functions, unoptimized images, scripts that block rendering.

The result is a site that may look great in design but takes 4 or 5 seconds to load. And every additional second of load time measurably reduces the conversion rate.

## The separation between content and presentation

The websites we build separate content from presentation logic. The final result the visitor sees is HTML, CSS, and very little JavaScript: static files served from a distribution network.

There is no database to query on every visit. No plugins to load. No attack surface for code injection.

## Speed and design are not the same thing

A website can be visually rich, with refined typography, smooth animations, and clear hierarchy, while loading in less than a second.

The key is when and how resources are loaded. A well-optimized image loaded at the right moment weighs no more than a poorly optimized one loaded on the first frame.

Design is a visual decision. Speed is a technical decision. They are independent, and treating them as if they were in conflict is the most common mistake in web projects.
