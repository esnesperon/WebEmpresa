When a company says it needs a corporate identity, the first thing it usually asks for is a logo. That is understandable: the logo is the most visible part of a brand. But the logo is just the tip of a much deeper iceberg.

A brand is not what it looks like. It is what it means.

## What builds a brand before the logo

Before designing anything, there are questions to answer that have little to do with design:

- Who exactly are you talking to?
- What problem do you solve for that person?
- How are you different from the alternatives they have?
- How do you want someone who just met you to describe you?

Without clear answers to these questions, any design decision is arbitrary. A color, a typeface, or a logo only make sense if they communicate something specific to someone specific.

## The visual system as a consequence

Once the strategy is clear, design flows much more easily. Colors reflect the brand's personality. Typography communicates the tone. The logo symbolizes all of the above in a condensed form.

When you work in that order, the result is coherent. When you start with the logo and build backwards, you almost always have to redo it.

## An identity that works across every channel

A well-built identity works equally on a business card, a social media post, an email signature, or the facade of a location. It does not depend on a specific file or a particular size.

That is called a visual system. And it is what differentiates a professional brand from a standalone logo.
